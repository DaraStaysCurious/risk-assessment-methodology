## Executive Summary

Every risk assessment begins with the same question: what could go
wrong here? The quality of everything that follows depends entirely
on how completely that question is answered.

This document maps OHS hazard identification practice — developed
across factory floors, oil rigs, and marine environments — onto GRC
threat identification methodology. It shows where the two disciplines
are structurally identical, and where OHS practice has developed
three techniques GRC consistently underuses: real-time risk
assessment, version-controlled living assessment tools, and
collaborative two-person inspection.

## What Hazard Identification Actually Is

**Effective hazard identification makes the invisible visible —
systematically, repeatedly, with enough rigor that familiarity
bias doesn't determine what gets found.**

The challenge isn't identifying obvious hazards. It's identifying
hazards that have become invisible through familiarity — the guard
removed "temporarily" six months ago, the unmarked chemical
container, the walkway everyone knows gets slippery but nobody
has formally flagged.

### Three Components

| Component | What it is | Why it matters |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|
| Structured systematic inspection | Defined route, versioned checklist, consistent schedule | Structure defeats familiarity bias — consistent route builds a mental baseline; deviations become visible |
| Collaborative observation | Rotating supervisor on every inspection | Fresh eyes catch what familiarity hides; supervisor has immediate authority to act |
| Living documentation | Version-controlled checklist, findings database | Patterns become visible over time; checklist evolves with the environment |

### How It Fails

| Failure mode | What it looks like |
|--------------|-------------------|
| Familiarity bias | The longer you've worked somewhere, the more invisible its hazards become |
| Checklist ossification | A checklist that never changes creates false confidence |
| Single-point observation | One inspector has blind spots — collaborative inspection is the fix |
| Normalcy bias | "Nothing has happened yet" mistaken for "nothing will happen" |
| Scope drift | Hazards at the edges — system interactions, specific conditions — get missed |

## What Threat Identification Actually Is

**GRC threat identification faces the identical challenge: surfacing
what could cause harm before it does, in an environment where
familiarity makes the most significant threats hardest to see.**

### GRC Vocabulary

| Term | Definition | OHS equivalent |
|------|-----------|---------------|
| Threat source | Anything with potential to trigger a threat event | Hazard |
| Threat event | The specific way a threat source causes harm | Hazard event |
| Vulnerability | A weakness a threat source can exploit | Unsafe condition |
| Predisposing condition | Organizational factors that increase likelihood | Environmental/organizational factor |

### Threat Source Categories

| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| Adversarial | External attackers, insider threats, nation-state actors |
| Accidental | Misconfigured servers, accidentally deleted databases |
| Structural | Hardware failure, software bugs, aging infrastructure |
| Environmental | Power outages, natural disasters, physical security failures |

### How GRC Threat Identification Fails

| Failure mode | GRC equivalent of OHS failure |
|--------------|-------------------------------|
| Over-reliance on documentation | Describes how systems should work — not how they do |
| Underweighting human threat sources | Technical scans don't find behavioral vulnerabilities |
| Generic threat intelligence | Accurate about the landscape, inaccurate about specific terrain |
| Single-assessor observation | No structural requirement for fresh eyes |
| Static assessment tools | Frameworks that don't evolve with the environment |
| Normalcy bias | "We've never been breached" treated as evidence of safety |

## The Complete Mapping

**Every element of OHS hazard identification has a direct GRC
equivalent.**

| OHS practice | GRC equivalent | Key insight |
|--------------|---------------|------------|
| Top-down inspection route | Structured scope traversal | Consistency of route produces consistency of coverage |
| Twice-daily inspections | Dual assessment cadence | Conditions change within cycles, not just between them |
| Rotating supervisor companion | Collaborative assessment | Fresh eyes catch what familiarity hides; authority enables immediate action |
| Version-controlled checklist | Living assessment framework | Tools that don't evolve create false confidence |
| Findings database | Risk register with longitudinal analysis | Patterns reveal systemic vulnerabilities point-in-time assessments miss |
| Near miss investigation | Security indicator analysis | Near misses are likelihood data — not reassurance |
| Ad-hoc finding response | Continuous risk management | Structured informality: fast containment, deliberate remediation |

### The Real-Time Finding Process

**When an unexpected hazard appears, the response is immediate
and structured — in OHS and GRC equally.**

| Step | OHS action | GRC equivalent |
|------|-----------|----------------|
| 1 | Notate immediately | Document the finding on the spot |
| 2 | Rapid collaborative triage | Assess with a second perspective |
| 3 | Cordon off if medium/high risk | Implement interim compensating control |
| 4 | Develop solution options | Prepare remediation options before escalating |
| 5 | Escalate with constraints noted | Present finding and options to decision-maker |
| 6 | Obtain authorization | Formal risk treatment authorization |
| 7 | Procure remediation resources | Acquire and deploy remediation |
| 8 | Hand off to operational owner | Transfer to accountable party for implementation |

**The two-speed principle:**

| Speed | Actions | Why |
|-------|---------|-----|
| Fast — minutes to hours | Identify, triage, contain | Every hour of unmanaged exposure costs |
| Deliberate — hours to weeks | Develop options, authorize, remediate, verify | Rushed remediation introduces new vulnerabilities |

## Living Assessment Tools

**An assessment tool that doesn't evolve with its environment
creates false confidence.**

| Practice | Why it matters |
|----------|---------------|
| Version number and date every checklist | Creates audit trail of how the tool evolved |
| Define revision triggers in advance | New systems, regulations, threat intel, post-incident review |
| Store version history | Shows how assessment evolved alongside the environment |
| Enter every finding into a longitudinal record | Turns individual findings into systemic insight |

**A risk register updated continuously tells you what your risk
landscape looks like right now. One refreshed at audit intervals
tells you what it looked like last time someone checked.**

## Implications for GRC Practice

1. **Define and follow a structured scope traversal route** —
   let the route, not attention, determine coverage.
2. **Build collaborative observation into assessments** — rotate
   reviewers, ensure fresh eyes at every significant activity.
3. **Design immediate escalation pathways** — define compensating
   control authority before it's needed.
4. **Treat near misses as seriously as incidents** — investigate,
   document, calibrate likelihood upward.
5. **Establish a real-time finding process** — define triage
   criteria, authority, and escalation pathways in advance.
6. **Version control your assessment tools** — define revision
   triggers, maintain history, update after environmental changes.
7. **Enter every finding into a longitudinal record** — patterns
   reveal what point-in-time assessments miss.

## Key Takeaways

- OHS hazard identification and GRC threat identification are
  structurally identical — different vocabulary, same challenge
- The most significant threats are often invisible in documentation
  but immediately visible to the people doing the work
- Collaborative observation — rotating fresh eyes — catches what
  familiarity makes invisible to a single assessor
- Near misses are likelihood data — not reassurance
- Assessment tools that don't evolve create false confidence
- Real-time risk assessment requires defined triage criteria and
  escalation pathways — defined before they're needed
- Documentation is not evidence of effectiveness — behavior is
